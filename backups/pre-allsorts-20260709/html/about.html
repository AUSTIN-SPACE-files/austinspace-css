Why solo

Why one specialist, not a team.

Working solo lets me focus on the work and the people I'm working for. The pace is set by the project, not by a billable-hours target. The variety is set by the clients I take on, not by what a sales team has sold. And the working week is built around getting the work done well, with enough flexibility to also have a life outside it.

What clients get from this model is consistency. The same person from the discovery conversation to the handover. The same person on the retainer two years later. No one to brief twice. No one to lose the brief. The site that ships is the site we agreed on.

You always know who's working on your site

No handoffs. No junior developers. The person you brief is the person who builds it.

Decisions happen faster

No approval chains, no weekly status calls. Things get done when they need to.

You're not paying for overhead

No offices, no account managers, no margin on top. Just the work.

I really care about the outcome

Every project gets my full attention. I take pride in the work, not just the delivery.

How I work

How this actually works

Direct communication

Straightforward conversations about what you need and how to get there. Email, video call, Asana, or whatever works for you.

Your project, your pace

Some clients want to be in every decision. Some want updates when there's something to update. Both work. The default is short, useful check-ins when something needs your input, and quiet otherwise.

Long after the launch

Most of my projects don't end at launch. The retainer picks up where the build leaves off. New pages, performance tuning, new content, the things that come up when the business changes. The relationship with the site is ongoing, and that's a feature.

Senior judgement, no middle layer

The decisions get made by the person doing the work. There's no junior to escalate from, no project manager to filter through, and no committee to align with. What you get is one person's opinion, and the experience to back it.

FAQ

Common questions

What makes you different from a typical freelancer?

The breadth of the experience. Ten years in environments where websites had to ship and perform, not just look good. Trainee designer through to Head of Design at BSI Merch, then Project Manager at a specialist Magento agency, now solo Squarespace work for SMEs. The skillset that came out of that covers wireframes, design, frontend development, SEO, analytics, and the operational thinking to put it all together. That's a different proposition from someone who's only ever worked on small projects.

What makes you different from an agency?

One person who understands the site inside out, zero agency overhead, and no account managers between you and the work. The output is comparable for most SME projects. The process is more direct, and the cost reflects that.

How did AUSTIN_SPACE come about?

A decade in eCommerce, ending as Head of Digital & Design at BSI Merch, then a couple of years as Project Manager at magic42, a specialist Magento agency. By the end of that, the case for solo Squarespace work was clear. The work itself is more rewarding when one person owns the whole thing, the client relationships are better when there's nothing between us, and the life outside of work is better when the working week doesn't include a daily commute or a billable-hours target.

What does working with you actually look like day to day?

Straightforward. You deal with one person from the first conversation through to launch, and through to the retainer that often follows. No handoffs, no briefing chains. The site that ships reflects an actual understanding of the business, rather than a brief passed between departments and gradually misinterpreted along the way.
